The Pipeline

Four stages, one destination

Stage 1 · 6th

Movement Foundations

Bodyweight mastery, landing school, sprint ABCs, games every session.

Stage 2 · 7th

Build The Athlete

Goblets, KBs, DBs on clean patterns. Pull-Up Ladder begins. Speed gets timed.

Stage 3 · 8th

Load The Patterns

The barbell year: PVC → bar → light load on front squat, RDL, bench.

Stage 4 · 9th

The Bridge Year

Full KPI baseline, then placement into the 2-Day All-Sport or Football 4-Day program.

Philosophy

Principles that govern every stage

Train Everything, Always

Every stage sprints, jumps, pushes, pulls, hinges, squats, and braces. The emphasis shifts by age; the menu never shrinks.

Movement Quality Buys Load

Load is never given by grade or birthday — it's purchased with clean reps. Standards from earlier stages never bend as weight goes up.

Speed Is A Skill

Sprinting appears in every stage with full recoveries. It's practiced like a skill, never used as punishment or conditioning.

Advance By Grade + Standards

Athletes move stages with their grade, and the standards check tells the coach what to regress. An 8th grader missing the hinge standard trains Stage 3 with Stage 2 loading on that pattern.

Fun Is A KPI

Stages 1–2 end every session in competition and games. The best program in the world fails if 6th graders stop showing up.

Growth Spurts Are Normal

During peak growth, coordination temporarily dips. Expect it, say it out loud to athletes and parents, re-teach patterns, and keep loads submaximal until the athlete owns their new limbs.

The Stages

Session templates & graduation standards

Two training days per week at every stage. Graduation standards are checked at the end of each year — athletes advance with their grade, and any missed standard is regressed on that pattern only.

Stage 1 · Movement Foundations

6th Grade

Learn to move, love to train. Own bodyweight positions, land quietly, sprint with intent, and want to come back. External load is limited to med balls and light implements — the barbell can wait, the movement patterns can't. Every session ends in competition because retention is the number-one KPI at this age.

Stage 2 · Build The Athlete

7th Grade

Add light load to clean patterns. Patterns earned in Stage 1 now get loaded with goblets, kettlebells, and dumbbells. The Pull-Up Ladder starts here. Speed becomes formal — timed flys enter the testing calendar. Still two games-and-competition touches every week, because they're still kids.

Stage 3 · Load The Patterns

8th Grade

The barbell year — earn it slow. Barbell introduction using the exact progression the high school room runs: PVC → empty bar → light load, with Front Rack Levels (R1→R4) governing the front squat. Nobody chases weight — the win is a technically clean bar. Athletes leave this stage ready to test as freshmen.

Stage 4 · The Bridge Year

9th Grade · Freshman

Test, place, and plug into the system. Weeks 1–2: full baseline KPI battery — vertical, broad, fly 10, 5-10-5, mile, plus bench and front squat heavy sets only for athletes who cleared Stage 3 standards. Then athletes plug directly into the existing Lawson programs: multi-sport athletes enter the 2-Day All-Sport Program; football players enter the 4-Day Off-Season on the Foundation track. Varsity track is earned at KPI weeks, never given.

Coaching non-negotiables

No 1RMs before Stage 4 — and no grinders at any stage, ever. Every session includes sprinting and jumping, fresh, with full recoveries. The standard beats the load: if depth, back position, or landing quality breaks, the weight comes down that day — no negotiation. Regress the pattern, not the athlete: a standards gap means one exercise moves back a level, not the whole kid repeating a stage. Track everything: every jump and fly goes in the athlete's record — it becomes their profile the day they enter the high school system.
